# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The primary users are IELTS training center teaching assistants and academic supervisors. They use the system during daily operations to enter students and teachers, schedule lessons, prevent timetable conflicts, prepare daily reminders, review individual student timetables, summarize teacher workloads, and export schedule data.

They are often working quickly between communication, scheduling, and administrative tasks, so the interface should support repeated use, fast scanning, and confident data entry.

## Product Purpose

This product is a local-first scheduling management system for IELTS training institutions. It exists to reduce manual scheduling effort, prevent teacher and student time conflicts, centralize class information, and make daily and monthly timetable exports easy to produce.

Success means assistants can reliably answer who has class, when, with whom, for how long, and whether a new class conflicts with existing plans without maintaining separate spreadsheets or calendars.

## Brand Personality

Professional, quiet, efficient, and polished. The system should feel trustworthy and calm, with enough visual refinement to be pleasant during daily use, but never ornamental at the expense of clarity.

The voice should be direct, helpful, and operational. Interface copy should prioritize clear task outcomes and actionable errors.

## Anti-references

The product should not look like a flashy marketing page, a student-facing mobile app, a childish education product, an over-carded dashboard, a raw spreadsheet clone, or a stiff government system.

Avoid decorative hero sections, playful consumer-app styling, excessive colors, unnecessary animation, oversized cards, and anything that makes routine administrative work feel slower.

## Design Principles

1. Make scheduling decisions obvious: show time, people, course type, duration, and conflicts with no hidden interpretation.
2. Optimize for repeated daily use: dense enough to scan, calm enough to keep open all day.
3. Keep trust higher than decoration: use consistent controls, clear states, and predictable navigation.
4. Preserve operational context: lists, filters, totals, and exports should match the way assistants actually communicate schedules.
5. Make errors actionable: conflict and validation messages should explain exactly who, when, and what blocks the action.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA as the practical baseline. Text contrast should be easy to read in Chinese, form controls should be keyboard reachable, focus states should be visible, and responsive layouts should avoid horizontal text overflow on smaller screens.

Motion should be minimal and state-driven, with reduced-motion support for any future animation. Color should not be the only indicator for errors, status, or selection.
